Assignment 2

The Municipality is worried that the canal- and support boats might pollute the air with their diesel engines. Since the Municipality wants to create ideal conditions for the swimmers so they can set records (always nice for PR), it is your task to figure out if their concern is warranted and what should or could be done to improve conditions. Next to that, Amsterdam wants to advertise this event as a Neutral Energy Event (NEE).-   

-   How many of the canal boats currently in use are diesel/fossil fuel driven and how many boats are electrical driven?
-   Are there peak times for the canal boats?
-   Try to compare the energy use of the canal boats to some other activities in the city.
-   Use canal boats more or less energy in relation to their carbon footprint compared to these other activities?
-   Would you consider it economically feasible?
-   How many support boats and vehicles are needed for the Paralympics event only.
-   If only clean energy can be used, how many solar panels or wind turbines are needed?
-   Is it possible to accommodate these facilities within the city?
-   Would their be any effect on the water quality if there are less/no canal boats using fossil fuels?

Results

To guide the event and its swimmers in the right canals, boats are needed to allow mobility on the water for the teams. These boats cannot disturb the swimmers nor the environment with pollutant exhausts. Furthermore, the event has to be energy-neutral.

Electrical boats

Currently, there are 7350 boats registered in the municipality of Amsterdam of which an estimate of 15% is emission-free. This is measured by looking at the vignettes handed out and corrected by taking samples by law enforcement on boats. Most of these boats are small (less than 6 meters in length), and of the bigger water vehicles (longer than 7 meters), less than 4% are electric (Municipality of Amsterdam. 2023a). However, by 2025 all boats have to be electric, meaning that a big switch will be made (Jacobs, et al., 2016).

Peak times
Peak times for boats are in July and August (weeks 27 to 35), but as seen in figure 1, the amount of boat hours goes up after January (week 5) and surpasses 1200 hours with easter holidays (week 15). Most boat hours are made on the weekend, specifically on Saturday. Boat hours for pleasure boats peak between 18:00 and 19:00, for passenger ships around 16:00, and there are no boats between 23:00 and 6:00). This data measured in the canals of Amsterdam by an Automatic Identification System (AIS-data), that looks into the use of the canals by manned boats (Municipality of Amsterdam, 2023a). Moreover, most boats are unmanned by a professional, and are for own use, as seen in Figure 2. However, the data seen in Figure 1 still gives a good implication for the use of boats throughout the year.

Energy use

Many boat hours mean many exhaust fumes from combustion engines. The retrofitting of an electric motor in existing boats is calculated by the municipality of Amsterdam (2023a) in their council letter, and averages 4000 euro for the most used boat: the small pleasure boat (the green bar in figure 2). The energy use then, is also estimated from 3 kW to 10 kW for bigger boats.

Currently, there are 7350 licensed boats in the Amsterdam canals, with power between 3 kW and 12 kW, as shown in table 2. Taking an average boat of 7 kW of 1000 kg (see table 2) that is fairing with an economic speed of 7 km/h for 6 hours (the whole day), this would mean that the amount of energy used is: 1000 kg * 1,1 * 6 hours = 6.6 kWh (Marinaut, n.d.). If all boats would farm on electric energy for a full day, this would take 6.6 kWh * 7 359 = 48 510 kWh.

This amount of energy can be compared to other big events that use a lot of electricity. For example, the Amsterdam Light Festival uses 15 000 kWh over the course of 53 days with 20 light installations (Amsterdam Light Festival, 2023), the boats thus use 3 times more energy than that festival. Another event that uses a lot of light, as well as sound, is a concert: the mean amount of energy used is 26 400 kWh (NPO Radio 2, 2015), which comes closer to the amount used by the canal boats. Far exceeding is a music festival such as Lowlands, which uses 300 000 kWh of electricity (Entertainment Business, 2015), which is 6 times more than the boats would use. Moreover, events like this have mostly a big carbon footprint due to the food produced, resources needed for food and drinks, and the transport needed to bring the materials, visitors, and artists to the location (NPO Radio 2 ,2015). This footprint is much bigger than the boat business can be.

Economic feasibility

For boat owners, the switch to electric motors is a must, since the municipality bans combustion motors in the city after 2025 (Jacobs, et al., 2016). The owner of Reederij Kooijman, who owns 32 tourist boats for city tours of more than 11 meters, admits that the retrofitting is a very expensive undertaking, as it costs around 250 000 euros per boat. However, the municipality mandates it, so the entrepreneur Kooijman has to, just like all the other canal boats (Wall, 2018). Though 11% of the boat owners in Amsterdam said to stop fairing their boat when it had to be fully electric, and 37% said to then allocate their fairing outside of the canals so they can keep using the combustion engine. Furthermore, 34% of the boat owners said to were ready to invest between 500 and 5000 euros to make the boat emission-free, and 44% said to not be ready to invest anything (Municipality of Amsterdam, 2022). That electric boats are cleaner, more relaxing to drive, and easier to upkeep, might be an unknown message still (Marinaut, n.d.).

Boats at the Paralympics event

In accordance with the Canadian guidelines for open-water swimming (2015), several escort boats are needed for feeding and tracking the athletes. They state that “gas fumes can be dangerous and annoying” (p. 6) and impede the swimmer, therefore the boats need to be electric indeed. For a course of 5 km, as in the case of the Amsterdam Paralympic canal swim, one boat per 500 meters is allowed, so 10 boats. The boats have to keep a safe distance from the swimmers and not run faster than 5 km per hour (Swimming Canada, 2015).

Clean energy

If for the event the boats should be powered with clean, renewable electricity, a certain amount of wind turbines or photo voltaic panels are needed. Electric boats of 5 meters, with a weight of 850 kilograms (including 4 people and the motor) with an external motor use 3kW, meaning a battery of 4 kWh is enough for fairing the whole day (with 4 kWh one can reach 30 kilometers in a speed of 7 km/h). The amount of kWh used for 10 boats, one day of fairing is then 850 kg * 1.1 * 6 hours * 10 boats = 56.1 kWh (Marinaut, n.d.).

According to the Dutch Central Bureau of Statistics (2023a) in the province of North Holland 12 wind turbines provided a normalized amount of 1835 million kWh in 2022. For one wind turbine daily this is 418 949 kWh of provided energy. One wind turbine then can power 7 467 times the electricity for the boats that day (418 949 / 56.1 = 7 467). This amount of energy is currently provided by both wind turbines on land and at sea in the energy region of North Holland (Province of North Holland, 2022b). In the city of Amsterdam, there are 28 wind turbines, as seen in figure 3, all located in the western harbor area. The plan is also to place at least three new wind turbines in this area, as the turbines are “fitting in the industrial surroundings of the western harbor area”, according to Sander Mager, manager at the local waterboard (Amsterdams Dagblad, 2022). Additionally, three wind turbines are to be placed in the Noorder IJplas, as the call for more renewable energy keeps growing and the resistance for health reasons too (van Zoelen, 2022). Accommodating this facility in the city is therefore possible but harder because of the complexity of installation. But because of the high return of electricity, wind turbines are a very good provider of clean energy, and therefore feasible.

As for solar panels. One solar panel generates about 0.8 kWh of electric energy per day (Dak Profijt, n.d.). Therefore, we need 0.8 kWh * 56.1 kWh = 45 solar panels running that day to provide enough electricity for the boats. According to data by the Province of North-Holland (2022a) in Amsterdam 717 Mega Joule = 199 kWh per inhabitant is provided in 2022 by sun energy every year, meaning that the boats in the event would take away the energy provided by the sun for 56.1 kWh / 199 = 0,2 inhabitants for a year. The placement of solar panels is currently considered easy, as roofs and walls support them and the municipality promotes the installation of new panels too (Municipality of Amsterdam, n.d.). Accommodating this facility in the city is therefore very possible and feasible.

Effect on water quality

The effect of internal combustion engine motors of boats on water quality in the Amsterdam canals is currently so bad that the municipality wants to ban diesel motors altogether (Jacobs, et al., 2016). This pollution exists out of noise pollution and carbonized fuel pollution (Morandin, et al., 2015). The latter is specified into the exhaust of particulate matter (PM10, PM2.5), Nitrogen oxide (NOx), and black carbon. This pollution from boats is a local source next to the traffic on roads (cars, trucks, etc) (Boogaard, et al., 2011). The effect on water quality if there are fewer boats using fossil fuels is mostly less deposition of Nitrogen oxide (Artz, et al., 2021).
For biodiversity, the biggest source of disturbance comes from the whirl of the propulsion of boats that causes the silt to swirl up and consequently block sunlight from hitting the canal foundation where water plants can thus not grow. High levels of nitrogen oxide have a similar effect, as algal blooms are prone to flourish in eutrophicated water (enrichment of the water with NOx), and then block sunlight from entering the water. Fish are attracted by water plants, and the lack thereof creates a very unattractive living area for aquatic organisms and low biodiversity (Municipality of Amsterdam, 2023b).
