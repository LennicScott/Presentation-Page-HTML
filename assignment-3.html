Assignment 3

Paris is hostng the Paralympics in 2024. One of the events at the Paralympics is an open water swimming event in the Seine (apparently the water is clean or so the French say). Since the City of Amsterdam thinks it is beter than Paris, they want to host an event before the Paralympics, snubbing the Parisians. The idea is to host a 5km. open water swimming event through the canals of Amsterdam. You are asked by the municipality of Amsterdam to advise on the feasibility of the event from the perspective of the safety of the partaking athletes from an environmental perspective. The event is going to be hosted in May.

For this exercise you need the data from this website. Download the Basis Bestand Gebieden Amsterdam (BBGA), both the data and the documentation. You already have the AirBnB data from Amsterdam from the workshop exercise.

The Municipality of Amsterdam is in a love hate relationship with AirBnb, see for example this and this article. Amsterdam wants to get a bit of insight in the number of tourists that will make use of AirBnB. Can you advise on or calculate for Amsterdam:

• What Amsterdam will receive from tourist tax if the event lasts a week and you will have 30.000 visitors?
• Plot the amount of AirBnB locations per neighbourhood.
• Which street in Amsterdam has the most AirBnB apartments?
• Try to cross reference the data from the AirBnB dataset with the BBGA. Can you figure out if all apartments of AirBnB are designated as housing? Which number of apartments are not rented out all the time but are also used as normal housing?
• How many hotel rooms should be built if Amsterdam wants to accommodate the same
number of tourists?

Results

The swimming event before the Paralympics will be so epic, that it will attract many visitors to Amsterdam. All these swimming enthusiasts will need to stay somewhere in the city. Naturally, people will stay in hotels, but alternative overnight stays will also be booked such as Airbnbs. Renting a room or apartment for a night via the platform Airbnb is increasingly popular after the COVID-19 crisis, visible in the growing amount of Airbnbs in Amsterdam, despite efforts of the municipality to limit the rentals (RTL Nieuws, 2023). Introduced restrictions are that the home cannot be rented out for more than 30 days per year and rental agents need a permit (Municipality of Amsterdam n.d.-a).

Financial profit for the city

Tourists in the city are a form of income for the municipality through tourist tax. The municipality receives different types of tax from hotels, campsites, and other forms of holiday rentals. In the case of a hotel, the municipality demands 3 euros per person per night, and 7% of the price of the chamber. Another form of rental is Airbnb. In the case of an Airbnb, the rental agent pays 10% of the turnover in tourist tax (Municipality of Amsterdam, n.d.-b).
It is interesting to calculate how much money from tourist tax the city will make from the event. The total amount depends on several factors.
First of all, the availability of Airbnbs at the time of the event which will be held right before the Paris Paralympics in August 2024. Based on data for one week in September this year (Inside Airbnb, 2023), the amount of available Airbnbs 8445, in total for 24401 persons, for an average price per listing of 258,76 per person per night. If the event would attract 30 000 visitors to Amsterdam, assuming they would fill up all the available Airbnbs of a week, would generate a turnover of 2.185.257,61 euro. The city takes 10% of turnover in tourist tax (Municipality of Amsterdam, n.d.-b), meaning that the city would make 218.525,76 euros from the Airbnbs.

However, there are still 5599 visitors left without accommodation. Assuming they will resort to a hotel, another calculation is needed to show the revenue from the hotels. There are 506 hotels in Amsterdam with 41 840 chambers and thus a capacity of 90 918 people (CBS, 2023). The average price of a hotel room in 2023 is 189 euros (Stil, 2023). Assuming that two people will share a room, 2800 rooms are let, generating (2800 * 189 euros * 7% (0,07)) + 5599 * 3 euros) = 53.841,- euros in tourist tax for the municipality.

So the total revenue for the municipality of Amsterdam for one week of accommodating 30.000 visitors assuming that the AirBnB’s will fill up first, is 218.525,76 + 53.841,00 =

€   272.366,76

All the Airbnbs in Amsterdam cluster mostly in the city centre, as visible in Figure 1. There are different amounts of Airbnbs per neighbourhood, as seen in Figure 2. The neighbourhood with the most Airbnbs .
The growing number of Airbnbs is signalling the popularity of certain neighbourhoods and streets, state Venerandi, et al. (2022) in research on the attractiveness of a neighbourhood expressed in the amount of Airbnbs. However, the researchers also underline the problematic side of Airbnb as renting out an apartment takes away (regulated) rental houses from the market and drives up the housing prices in cities. This would be the case if regular rental houses were rented out full-time as Airbnbs. As mentioned before, according to the regulations of the Municipality of Amsterdam, people's houses can only be rented out as Airbnbs for 30 days a year, so it should not be possible for apartments to be rented out full-time and be listed as housing instead of holiday rental (Municipality of Amsterdam, n.d.-a). It is interesting to see whether this is the case, with the available data.
The available data on this comes from the listings of Airbnb (Inside Airbnb, 2023) and the municipality of Amsterdam itself: the base file for areas in Amsterdam (Basis Bestand Gebieden Amsterdam) (Municipality of Amsterdam, 2023-b).

Hotel rooms

To accommodate all the visitors coming to Amsterdam, many places for overnight stays are needed. Between Airbnb and regular hotels, there is a difference in the license (Municipality of Amsterdam, n.d.-b), the social and financial impact on the city (Venerandi, et al., 2022), and overall business model. Currently, there are two trends: less support for Airbnb by local governments (RTL Nieuws, 2023) and a growing number of tourists in the city (Stil, 2023). Therefore, for the plausible scenario of the ban on Airbnb, there are more hotel rooms needed to host all the (new) visitors in Amsterdam and it is relevant to calculate how many.

Currently, Airbnb can accommodate 15 360 people (Inside Airbnb, 2023). Again, there are 90 918 hotel beds in 41 840 rooms in Amsterdam (CBS, 2023), meaning (90 918 / 41840 = ) 2.17 beds per room. If then all the Airbnbs were gone, there would be (15 360 / 2.17 = ) 7 078 hotel rooms needed. The average number of rooms per hotel in Amsterdam is 82.69 (CBS, 2023), meaning there will be (7 078 / 82.69 = ) 86 new hotels needed.

This means that 86 different licences should be issues.
